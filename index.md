I am a computational biologist focused on developing scalable computational tools, analytical methods, and data infrastructure to support genomics research and human health. My work spans cancer genomics, immunotherapy, structural variation, machine learning, and sequencing analysis, with experience building production software, cloud-based genomics workflows, and reproducible computational frameworks for biological data.

I currently work as a Computational Biologist at [Benthic Genomics](https://www.benthic.bio/), where I develop and maintain production genomics tools and infrastructure, support sequencing workflows across cloud and cluster computing environments, improve data provenance and sample-tracking systems, and collaborate closely with wet-lab scientists to enable new targeted sequencing applications.

Previously, I worked at [Phase Genomics](https://phasegenomics.com/), where I advanced from Bioinformatics Research Analyst to Senior Data Scientist. There, I developed machine learning methods for detecting structural variants from proximity ligation sequencing data, built reusable frameworks for model development and evaluation, and helped create automated workflows that transformed raw sequencing data into actionable biological insights in cancer and reproductive genetics.

My path into computational biology began at the University of Oregon, where I earned a Master of Science in Biology through the Applied Bioinformatics and Genomics Master's Program. During graduate school, I discovered a passion for using computational methods to answer biological questions with direct relevance to human disease. Through an internship at Oregon Health & Science University with Dr. Reid Thompson and subsequent work with his team at the [Portland VA Research Foundation](https://www.pvarf.org/staff_investigators/reid-thompson/), I helped develop software and analytical approaches for studying cancer immunotherapy response, neoantigen prediction, and tumor genomics.

Before transitioning into bioinformatics, I conducted research in evolutionary biology at the University of Oregon's Institute of Ecology and Evolution. Working with [Drs. Bradshaw and Holzapfel](https://bradshaw-holzapfel-lab.uoregon.edu/), I studied the evolution of blood feeding in the pitcher plant mosquito, with interest in its implications for vector-borne disease transmission. Although my scientific focus has evolved since then, that experience sparked the curiosity about biology, disease, and scientific research that continues to drive my work today.

Both in academia and industry, I have been motivated by the same goal: applying scientific approaches to better understand biology and ultimately improve human health. Whether developing bioinformatics software, maintaining genomics infrastructure, analyzing sequencing data, or building machine learning models, I enjoy solving complex problems at the intersection of biology, data, and technology.

To learn more, do not hesitate to reach out to me or explore my work through the links on the sidebar!
